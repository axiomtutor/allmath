Symbols:

Question: What is the right way to reason?

Answer: This is a philosophical question, and we will not attempt to answer it in its totality. But having some scruples about how a good argument works is still a good idea. It's hard to know where to start.

Question: What did old, dead, mostly white men say about this?

The ancient Greeks, Hindus, and Chinese all have philosophers in their intellectual traditions who studied Logic. For much of the early history of the topic, people focused on which arguments were good, and what made them good. Appeals to authority, verifiability, coherence in a network of beliefs, all seemed to make a showing in one way or another. Aristotle had a particularly interesting analysis of logic, as it was comprehensive, focused, organized, and the most startling feature was that--almost in anticipation of modern studies in Logic thousands of years later--it always had a eye toward describing the features of the logical system itself, rather than only describing practical use of the logical system in individual arguments. This is so-called "meta-theory", or the theory of the theory, which is the primary subject of modern Mathematical Logic.

I would love to give a wider view of history and philosophical authorities, but for the sake of staying on-target, Aristotle really the best pick if we're going to pick just one. He's an old, dead, white man, but he was really on to something.

Many different philosophers and traditions focused on describing the features that make a good argument. Appeal to authority, verifiability, coherence in a network of beliefs, all made some kind of appearance in some philosophers' meditations on Logic. However, in Aristotle's work the single central notion was deduction: Moving from one set of sentences to a new sentence justified by those before it. The canonical example is

(1) All men are mortal.

(2) Socrates is a man.

(3) Therefore, Socrates is mortal.

The first two sentences are premises. They supply justification for the third sentence, called the conclusion. Forgive the gendering, times were different then, back when a men were men except sometimes when they were part horse.

In Aristotle's vocabulary this is called a syllogism, what today we call a deduction. He distinguished deduction from another "good" form of argument, induction. Aristotle characterizes induction as "argument from the particular to the universal", for example, by observing fish and their ability to swim, therefore concluding that all fish swim. Deduction is distinguished for Aristotle as argument from the universal ("All men are mortal") to the particular ("Socrates is mortal").

Today we would characterize induction by its (greater? more direct or narrow?) use of empirical evidence and probabilistic reasoning; deduction would be characterized as more mathematical, involving reasoning that is either independent of evidence. We would characterize the inference from x+1 = 2 to x=1 as deductive; the inference from "every fish I've observed can swim" to "fish swim" is inductive. I'm tip-toeing over some philosophical ground with these parentheses that maybe I should run a little rougher over. Hopefully the distinction between induction and deduction is clear, at least in their modern uses. Of course, we will be studying deduction for now. Once we understand Probability better, we'll make some slow and hard-fought gains in understanding induction--in my opinion induction is both extremely difficult to analyze in a useful way, and yet also an extremely important feature of reason.

Much of Aristotle's deductions studied the predication of objects. So much fancy-talk here, but objects are just the things we talk about. In sentence (2) above, we predicated of Socrates that he is a man. Socrates was the object, "is a man" was the predicate. Predicates are like properties, and objects have properties. However, some predicates are not exactly the kinds of things we would regard as a property, like "is at home"--maybe we would call this an "accidental property" rather than an "intrinsic property". In the sentence "the water molecule has an oxygen atom" there are two objects, the water molecule and the oxygen atom. The predicate "has a/an" connects these two things. We usually wouldn't think of "has a/an" is a property, but here we use it as a predicate, or a relation--we regard properties and relations as very similar sorts of things.

Statements involving predicts can be of a particular, like "Socrates is a man", but they can also be of universals, like "all fish swim" or "all men are mortal". These are so-called "all" sentences. The sentence "someone ate the cookies" is taken to be equivalent to "at least one person ate the cookies"--the speaker could intend to mean that exactly one person ate the cookies, but could also mean that two or three or a hundred people did, so we will assume the latter. In any case, this "some" sentence is about a particular (or particulars), even though no specific object is mentioned. There are also "no" sentences, "not every", and "some not".

"all": All men are mortal.

"some": Someone ate the cookies.

"not all": Not all cats are orange.

"not some": Not one fish can read.

"all not": All men are not fish.

"some not": Some men are not women.

So much variety. Can we find unity? Notice that some of these are actually a bit superfluous. "All men are mortal" is the same as saying "Not one man is immortal." Aristotle delivered the "square of opposition" wherein sentence forms that are diagonal from each other are contrary to each other.

__Example__

Here's an example of how this analysis helps us. Suppose two people are in a political argument and one says "Every law should be obeyed." The other responds, "Just yesterday you went 5 miles-an-hour over the speed limit." The former says "Yeah, that's different." This effectively has the form "All A's are B" as this is the form of "All laws are obligatory" (rephrasing to make A and B obvious). To which the other person asserts "x is A but not B", which is "the speed limit law is a law that is not obligatory". The translation into the official form is not perfect, but presumably the interlocutors would both agree with this sentence and that it's the point behind saying "you went 5 miles-an-hour over". The second person giving justification for the sentence "something is A but not B" which is in contradiction to the first sentence, "all A is B".

Now the first person is, with "that's different", either trying to weasel out of full commitment to their first premise, or said more politely, trying to qualify precisely which laws she has in mind. But if that's so, she needs to do a better job of articulating exactly which laws she has in mind, before she can suppose that she has adequately made her point and reasonably expect others to comply with the principle.

Question: What is the point here? Where is this going?

Answer: This has been a quick--so quick as to be almost non-existent--description of ancient studies in Logic. I just want to give a taste so that the reader can contrast and observe how modern Logic is different. This can help you to see what it is that we're trying to do in modern Logic. Modern Logic is symbolic and abstract in the way that Math is.

Pre-symbolic analysis was largely informal and philosophical in nature. Aristotle elevated the level of formalism and organization of the topics, but not to the level of a full-blown mathematical study. After Aristotle, there were no deep sea changes in what people thought about Logic. The medieval Europeans and Islamic scholars did further some investigations, particularly in the topic of "modal logic". Modal logic was the study of necessary and accidental properties, largely motivated by religious and philosophical questions. However, about 2,000 years after Aristotle's writings, philosopher Immanuel Kant expressed a sentiment that was at least roughly shared by a large number of his contemporaries when he said that little if anything in the field of Logic has been improved since Aristotle.

However, Kant was writing pretty close to the cusp of a future movement. Mechanization of labor had been world-changing to Europe--especially in the wake of bubonic plague, wiping out much of the labor force and creating the need for a small number of people to perform a large amount of labor. In the use of better scientific technology to build ships and then navigate them, Europe brutally plundered the world over and profited wildly at the time. This was largely seen by Europeans at the time as an unqualified good. Maps were physical devices that encoded information, and astrolabes did something like calculation for navigators. The philosopher and mathematician Leibniz, co-inventor of Calculus, grew up in this scientific and technical Europe, and wondered whether machines could not just do physical labor but also mental labor. He imagined a thinking machine that could solve Math problems with the turning of gears, or possibly other physical mechanisms. He was anticipating the computer, and even was responsible for the construction of some non-digital computers that could perform simple arithmetic. These would eventually evolved into slide rules. Slide rules, if you're not familiar with them, are super awesome non-digital calculators that were common place in American high schools in the 1950s. You should go buy and play with one right damned now, they are fascinating.

Not long after Leibniz came George Boole, also a mathematician and philosopher, interested in a mathematical study of Logic and Probability. He came to realize that there is almost an arithmetic of truth--a tantalizing way of stating something that actually is a little more mundane than you might imagine. If you know that sentences A and B are true but C is false, then the sentence "A and B or C" can be "calculated" from knowing the truths of the component sentences, kind of like how you can calculate x+yz from knowing the numeric values of x, y, and z. When, decades later, Frege formalized this fully in a calculus of sentences, and further decomposed the sentences into predicates and objects, the study of Logic had reached a very serious milestone.

Up to this point the mathematicians had mostly ignored the formal study of Logic, regarding it I think as a kind of interesting, vaguely mathematical, but not truly mathematical study. After Frege, the mathematicians started piling on in a serious way, beginning with Russell, crossing paths with Turing and the development of modern computers, and flourishing into the fairly cutting-edge and large field that it is today. The mathematical study of logic today asks a lot of questions about the properties of logical systems, what kinds of sentences can be proved in them, and so on. In most of these conversations, the distinction between syntax and semantics is crucial. The syntax is the dead, meaningless symbols used in a language, and it also describes the rules for composing symbols together to form "well-formed expressions" and "sentences". The semantics is the "meaning" (in some sense) of those dead, material, finite symbols--the semantics is robust, live, mysterious, and floating in the ether. Ok, that's a little dramatic, but the point is that we do our best to capture the semantics in a syntactic system, and the study of Logic is often concerned with just how well we do that.

So there you have it, a lightning history and a flurry of name-dropping. Now we will start Symbolic Logic.
