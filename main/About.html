Introduction

This is an attempt to explain all of modern Mathematics, targeted at an intellectually mature reader. Of course discussing all of modern Mathematics is impossible, but by approximating infinity maybe we will get somewhere. By "intellectual maturity" I mean the ability and willingness to think abstractly, to be in a state of uncertainty and work through it, and the self-discipline to do research.

Throughout I try to motivate investigations with natural questions, sometimes with natural physical, statistical, or other interpretations. Unlike in most Math texts where the questions and exercises can be skipped and still you will find a logical development in the rest of the text--in this website, the mathematical content is entirely in the answers to questions and exercises, so that no question can be skipped without missing the logical flow of ideas. When you read a question I intend for the reader to think for at most a few minutes, make some sincere attempt at an answer, whether they can or cannot answer the question, then check my answer. Exercises require less originality than that but often lead to insights that cannot be skipped either. Definitions, theorems, and more are contained in answers to questions.

The emphasis here is on explanation and understanding, and the reader is encouraged to answer the questions and give explanations, if only to oneself. I also use software as a calculator and illustrator, especially the Python programming language and Geogebra software, both of which are free. If you're not familiar with the basics of programming in Python I encourage starting with that section--although I don't mean to teach Python programming, much of this website will be hard to follow without reproducing the Python examples. Every section, from Arithmetic and thereafter, assumes that you have seen the programming prerequisites linked above. It is a very fast and basic introduction, so if you're comfortable with Python you can skip it.

The structure is to start at the very beginning with Arithmetic and simple representation in base-10. The section on Arithmetic progresses through standard operations and their closures until we reach complex numbers and basic arithmetic with them. We progress to modular arithmetic and advanced counting, stopping shy of a full course on Number Theory and Combinatorics. From there we progress through Logic, Algebra, Geometry, and Discrete Math. At this point, the particular sequence of subjects becomes less important and a choose-you-own-adventure is possible. At the start of each topic I indicate the prerequisites.
