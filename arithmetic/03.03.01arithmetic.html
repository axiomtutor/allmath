Chapter 03: Extensions

Section 03: Matrices

Abstract: Tuples are ordered lists of numbers, and matrices are ordered lists of tuples. They often represent points in space and operations on points, although it is also very helpful to think of matrices as encoding the composition of linear transformation. We define the sum and product, and magnitude of each.

Terms: Tuple, matrix, row, column, dimension, coordinate, dot-product, square matrix, permutation matrix, main diagonal, off diagonal, determinant, minor, singular matrix.

Symbols: [...], [[...], ..., [...]], Mt,

Question: Ordered lists of numbers come up all the time in Math and Science. In Math they can tell you the coordinates of a point (a concept to be defined later, but presumably every reader is at least a little familiar with the idea); in Physics they can indicate the direction and size of a force; in Computer Science they can indicate the red-green-blue values of a pixel; and in Statistics they can represent a list of measurements. These are just a few examples and many more are possible.

We will call these lists tuples. The numbers in the list could be integers, rationals, reals, complex, integers mod some number, or p-adic numbers. Although the choice of numbers is flexible, the most interesting choice that I know of is the complex numbers. Also, the choice is not perfectly flexible: For theoretical reasons we will not consider the natural numbers as an acceptable number set.

Here are three examples of tuples:

t1 = [1, 2, 3]

t2 = [3, 4, 5]

t3 = [8, 9, 10, 11, 12]

The length of t1 is 3, and same for t2. Of course t3 has length 5. It is often useful to collect lists together, ultimately forming a list of lists. We will always require the lists to have the same length. The list of lists we call a matrix.

M = [t1, t2] = [ [1,2,3], [3,4,5] ]

Notice that the matrix has length 2. When a list is inside of a matrix, we call it a row. The reason is because we typically write the matrix M in the following way to help our eyes see the information more naturally:

$$M = \left[\begin{matrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 3 & 4 & 5 \end{matrix}\right]$$

In this presentation a matrix is a rectangular grid of numbers, and when we write it this way it is standard to omit commas separating the values--just for brevity. A row is then seen to also be a "horizontal slice" of the data. We also define a column as a vertical slice of data, such as in this case [1, 3]. That's not exactly right because saying [1,3] forgets that this data was oriented vertically rather than horizontally, something that we sometimes want to remember. Hence we write that the column is either $$\left[\begin{matrix}1\\ 3\end{matrix}\right]$$ or to keep things in-line we may write [1,3]t. The super-script t suggests the "transpose", something we'll talk more about soon. We additionally define the dimension of a matrix to be a pair of numbers written in the form m×n so that for instance the example M above has dimension 2×3. Of course the left number reports the number of rows and the right number reports the number of columns. It used to be a helpful mnemonic to call this "RC cola" in order to remember which comes first, rows or columns--but kids these days grumble grumble.

We can often harmlessly forget that matrices are lists of lists, and instead just regard them as rectangles of numbers. In doing so we also blur the distinction between a tuple and a matrix because a tuple is a rectangle of numbers with height 1. That is to say, a row can be regarded as a 1×n matrix where n is the length of the row. We will also say that a column is the same thing as a m×1 matrix.

How can we represent matrices in Python? Does SymPy do better?

Answer: In bare Python the representation is obvious, we just write t1 = [1, 2, 3] to get a tuple. However, the tuple operations are not what we usually want in Mathematics. Try [1,2,3] + [3,4,5] and see what returns. Often in Math what we wish would return is [4,6,8] instead. To get a matrix we can of course write mat1 = [ [1,2,3], [4,5,6] ], but the same problem applies with addition--the result is not what we typically desire. In this construction, notice, matrices and rows are fundamentally different. If you access t1[0] you will get a number whereas if you access mat1[0] you get a row. mat1[0][1] will produce a number because it selects the "zeroth" (or first) row and then the "oneth" (or second) number.

In SymPy everything is a matrix, rather than having a distinction between rows or columns and matrices. Hence the following script prints a matrix. Notice that in SymPy a single row input is automatically converted to a column. This is because, by convention, we use columns more frequently than rows. There is no deep or interesting reason why, it is a fairly arbitrary choice.

1| import sympy as sp

2| m = sp.Matrix( [[1,2,3], [3,4,5]] )

3| v = sp.Matrix( [-1,0,1] )

4| w = sp.Matrix( [[1j, -1+2j, 2-2j]] )

5| print(m)

6| print(v)

7| print(w)

8| print(m.shape)

9| print(v.shape)

10| print(w.shape)

Notice the difference between v and w. v was entered as a single list, and SymPy automatically interpreted this as a column, whereas a list containing just one list like w was interpreted as a row.

Question: It is easy enough to suppose that matrix addition should be coordinate-wise. E.g.

$$\left[\begin{matrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 3 & \_2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \end{matrix}\right] + \left[\begin{matrix} \_2 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & \_2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 \end{matrix}\right] = \left[\begin{matrix} \_1 & 5 & 3 \\ 3 & \_4 & 2 \\ 2 & 2 & 1\end{matrix}\right]$$

Notice by the way that the sum of two matrices is not defined if they do not share exactly the same dimensions--i.e. they have to have exactly the same number of rows and columns for this to be meaningful. If we were to guess the right way to multiply a matrix times a simple number, the guess would again be right.

$$-2\left[\begin{matrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6\end{matrix}\right] = \left[\begin{matrix} -2 & -4 & -6 \\ -8 & -10 & -12\end{matrix}\right]$$

However, what should the product of two matrices be? The same coordinate-wise product so that, e.g.

$$\left[\begin{matrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 3 & \_2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \end{matrix}\right] \cdot \left[\begin{matrix} \_2 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & \_2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 \end{matrix}\right] \stackrel{?}{=} \left[\begin{matrix} \_2 & 6 & 0 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \end{matrix}\right]$$

Answer: This turns out not to be the version of multiplication that is most useful, surprisingly. Instead, a much more complicated version of multiplication turns out to be the right thing to do for most (but not all) applications.

To explain the correct version of multiplication it is best to start with what is called the dot-product. The dot-product is an operation that we perform between a row on the left and a column on the right. This operation will turn out to be of absolutely profound importance, although the reason is theoretical. Because we are here focused on computation, not theory, the reasons why this fairly simple operation is so important must remain a mystery until we study it further in Linear Algebra.

In any case, the dot-product is performed by coordinate-wise multiplying the row and column, and then summing over this result. In order to break this operation into steps, let's agree to say that cw is the coordinate-wise product of a row and a column. Therefore

$$[1\,\, 2\,\, 3]cw\left[\begin{matrix}\_2 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{matrix}\right] = [\_2\,\, 0, 3]$$

The dot-product is then the sum over this coordinate-wise product. Therefore

$$[1\,\, 2\,\, 3]\cdot \left[\begin{matrix}\_2\\ 0 \\1\end{matrix}\right] = \_2 + 0 3= 1$$

Notice that, very importantly, the dot product is not the kind of thing you usually think is a good multiplication operation. By multiplying two matrices, one row and one column, you get out a number. You would typically think that multiplication of two things would result in the same kind of thing. Still, this is the right thing to do.

Note by the way that we cannot perform the dot-product on rows and columns that do not have the same length.

Question: Now that we understand the dot-product on rows and columns, how do we multiply matrices in general?

Answer: Let's think of this coordinate-by-coordinate in the product $$\left[\begin{matrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 3 & \_2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \end{matrix}\right] \cdot \left[\begin{matrix} \_2 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & \_2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 \end{matrix}\right] \stackrel{?}{=} \left[\begin{matrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i \end{matrix}\right]$$ Typically I should write these coordinates as ai,j but that's ugly and tedious. Coordinate a will be calculated by finding the dot-product of the first row and column of the two matrices. We've already computed this in the previous question and found that the value was 1. The coordinate b will be calculated by finding the dot-product of the first row and second column of the two matrices, i.e.

$$b = [1\,\, 2 \,\, 3]\cdot \left[\begin{matrix}3 \\ \_2 \\ 1 \end{matrix}\right] = 3 - 4 + 3 = 2$$

Of course c is found as the dot-product between the first row and third column of the two matrices, and d is found as the dot of the second row and first column, and so on.

If you multiply these two matrices you should find that their product is

$$\left[\begin{matrix} 1 & 2 & 5 \\ \_5 & 14 & \_1 \\ \_2 & 1 & 1 \end{matrix}\right]$$

Question: Suppose we product an m×n matrix with a p×q matrix, i.e. these are the dimensions of the two matrices. Under what conditions will the matrix product be possible to compute? What will be the dimension of the resulting matrix?

Answer: Everything is defined in terms of the dot-product and the dot-product must be performed on rows with the same length as the column. Since n is the length of each row in the left matrix and p is the length of each column in the second matrix (if you thought these were reversed, remember: m is the NUMBER of rows, not the length of the row) then we need to have n = p. In that case the product will be defined.

The result will have dimension m×q. That is because, for every row of the first matrix there will be a row of values in the product; and for every column in the second matrix, there is a column in the product.

We will define a square matrix as any matrix with the same number of rows as columns, i.e. it will have dimension n×n for some positive integer n. Obviously any two square matrices can be added and they can be multiplied.

Exercise: We have seen that the dot-product of a row and column is a number. Is it possible to compute the matrix product of a column and a row?

Answer: Yes! We have seen that matrices of dimensions m×n and p×q can always be multiplied so long as n=p. If we have a column on the left and a row on the right, then n=1=p so this will always be possible. The result of the product will be a m×q matrix.

$$\left[\begin{matrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{matrix}\right]\cdot [4\,\, 5] = \left[\begin{matrix}4 & 5 \\ 8 & 10 \\ 12 & 5\end{matrix}\right]$$

In computing this, every dot-product that is computed is between a row of length 1 and a column of length 1. With this example you can kind of see what the matrix product is doing from two new perspectives. From one perspective we took the column $$\left[\begin{matrix}1\\2\\3\end{matrix}\right]$$ copied it, and multiplied one copy by 4 and multiplied the other copy by 5.

From the dual perspective, so to speak, you could also imagine that we took the row [4 5] and copied it three times, multiplying each copy by 1 and then 2 and then 3. Thinking of matrix operations in this way can be a useful shortcut in computation.

We can do something similar for general matrix multiplication, but it's more complicated and not quite as helpful. But just to illustrate, take the previous example,

$$\left[\begin{matrix}1+i & 1-i \\ 2-3i & 0 \end{matrix}\right]\cdot \left[\begin{matrix}0 & i \\ \_i & 2i\end{matrix}\right]$$

We can get the first column of the product by multiplying

$$0\left[\begin{matrix}1+i\\2-3i\end{matrix}\right] = \left[\begin{matrix}0\\0\end{matrix}\right]$$

and then summing that with

$$\_i\left[\begin{matrix}1-i\\0\end{matrix}\right] = \left[\begin{matrix}\_1-i \\ 0\end{matrix}\right]$$

so that the first column of the product is

$$\left[\begin{matrix}1-i \\ 0\end{matrix}\right]$$

The second column is found with

$$i\left[\begin{matrix}1+i\\2-3i\end{matrix}\right] = \left[\begin{matrix} \_1+i \\ 3+2i\end{matrix}\right]$$

summing with

$$ 2i\left[\begin{matrix}1-i\\0\end{matrix}\right] = \left[\begin{matrix}2+2i \\ 0\end{matrix}\right] $$

so that the second column is

$$\left[\begin{matrix}1+3i\\3+2i\end{matrix}\right]$$

Tacking these together you get the same matrix as before. You could likewise find a way to tack rows together.

One way that this is particularly useful, though, is with a sparse matrix, i.e. one that is mostly 0's.

Question: What is the absolute value of a matrix?

Answer: This one doesn't really have an answer, and you might have suspected that it wouldn't. However, there is a near-by concept that we frequently use, called the determinant of a matrix. For this we still use the bars around the matrix, sort of like the absolute value. The determinant is only defined for square matrices, and it is easiest to understand by first looking at a 2×2 matrix.

$$\left| \left[ \begin{matrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{matrix}\right]\right| = 1\cdot 4 - 2\cdot 3 = \_2$$

The main diagonal of a matrix is frequently useful and so we will define it now. The main diagonal of a matrix is the set of coordinates ai,j. That is to say that the main diagonal is the set of coordinates just as far out along the row as it is along the column. This could also be said: The main diagonal is the set of coordinates starting in the upper-left and traversing down to the lower-right corner. Similarly we define the off-diagonal to be the set of coordinates that start at the lower-left and traverse up to the upper-right.

__Example__

We can then say that the determinant of a 2×2 matrix is found by multiplying the main and off diagonals, and then subtracting (in that order).

That seems simple enough but it gets dramatically harder with a 3×3. To compute the determinant of a 3×3 matrix we need to find the matrix minors. The minors are all of those matrices which we could obtain by deleting a row and a column. Let's take the example

$$\left[\begin{matrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4&5&6\\7&8 &9\end{matrix}\right]$$

The minor of a1,1 is matrix formed by deleting the first row and first column (where a1,1 lies at the intersection of these).

$$M_{1,1} = \left[\begin{matrix}5&6 \\ 8&9\end{matrix}\right]$$

Similarly

$$M_{2,2} = \left[\begin{matrix}1 & 3 \\ 7&9\end{matrix}\right]$$

We also need to know the sign of the minor. The sign of the minor is easily described by the following diagram:

$$\left[\begin{matrix}+ & - & + \\ - & + & - \\ + & - & +\end{matrix}\right]$$

This diagram tells you that the sign of M1,1 is + and the sign of M1,2 is -, and so on. Finally we can say what the determinant of this 3×3 matrix is. We may pick any row or any column, and combine every coordinate along it with its signed minor. In this example, if we pick the first row, we get

$$\left| \left[\begin{matrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4&5&6\\7&8 &9\end{matrix}\right]\right| = a_{1,1}M_{1,1} - a_{1,2}M_{1,2}+a_{1,3}M_{1,3}$$

And this is

1(5*9-8*6) - 2(4*9-7*6) + 3(4*8-7*5) = 45-48-2(36-42)+3(32-35) = -3-2(_6)+3(_3) = -3+12-9 = 0

Because the matrix has a determinant of 0 we call this a singular matrix.

The following script will compute the same thing.

1| import sympy as sp

2| m = sp.Matrix( [[1,2,3],[4,5,6],[7,8,9]] )

3| print( sp.det(m) )

Question: How do we calculate the determinant of an n×n matrix if n > 3?

Answer: The pattern is basically by "recursion", a concept I don't want to get into quite yet, but you can still pick up the idea by seeing examples. We just expand again along a row or column, except this time the minor of each coordinate will be a determinant of a 3×3 instead of a 2×2.

Let's take an example with a lot of 0's to keep things easy.

$$\left|\left[\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & \_1 \\ 0 & 2 & \_ 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 & 1\end{matrix}\right]\right|$$

Of course we will again use the diagram of signs

$$\left[\begin{matrix}+&-&+&-\\-&+&-&+\\+&-&+&-\\-&+&-&+\end{matrix}\right]$$

Expanding along the first row, since two of the coordinates are 0 we can forget them and just focus on 1M1,1 - _1M1,4. In order not to clutter the explanation I won't write out the computation of these, but simply say that M1,1 = 4 and M1,4 = 0. Therefore the determinant of this 4×4 is 4.

Exercise: Re-compute the determinant above by expanding along some other row or column.

Answer: Of course, 4.

Exercise: Compute the determinant of a "diagonal" matrix. A diagonal matrix is one that has only zeros in coordinates that are not the diagonal. For instance, compute

Question: How can you compute the determinant of an "upper triangular" matrix? An upper triangular matrix is one with zeros entirely below the main diagonal, like for instance

$$\left|\left[\begin{matrix}1&2&3\\0&4&5\\0&0&6\end{matrix}\right]\right|$$

Answer: Again, it's the product of the main diagonal elements, again because expanding in the right way makes other minors cancel. In this example it's 24.

Note: As a final note, we will sometimes have use for the transpose of a matrix. We've already seen the transpose of a row, turning it into a column. No surprise: The transpose of a matrix does the same thing to all of its rows. Another way to describe it is that it "flips along its main diagonal":

$$ \left[\begin{matrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{matrix}\right]^t = \left[\begin{matrix}1 &4\\ 2&5 \\ 3&6 \end{matrix}\right]$$
