Section 04: Complex numbers

Abstract: The complex numbers close the reals under square-roots. We investigate complex number parts and their relationship to 2-dimensional space.

Terms: Complex number, standard form, real part, imaginary part, conjugate, complex plane, magnitude, argument, subset

Symbols: ℂ, ℜ, ℑ, Arg(.), ⊆

Question: Since we have not yet actually closed our system under square-roots, what will we get when we do?

Answer: The most exciting number system yet! The reals, let's face it, were kind of dull if necessary to get the full number line. They just kind of continue everything that we could have just by using "close enough" rational numbers. And we already have all square-roots of positive numbers, since these just have the usual decimal sequences, so we make no gains there by closing our system under roots. Our gains come from considering sqrt(_1). This number we call i.

Of course there are other numbers that result from this like sqrt(_2) but this is just the same as sqrt(_1 * 2) = sqrt(_1) sqrt(2) = i sqrt(2). So it seems that we don't need square-roots of other negative numbers. What about cube-roots? Well cbrt(_1) = _1 so no gain has been made there, and for a fourth root, $$\sqrt[4]{\_1} = (\_1)^{\wedge 1/4} = ((\_1)^{\wedge 1/2})^{\wedge 1/2} = i^{\wedge 1/2}$$ so again we can suffice with just i.

We will eventually prove that every complex number can be written in standard form, which is a + ib where a, b ∊ ℝ. This seems unlikely since there is no obvious way to write i^1/2 in such a way, but we will find a way. In the mean time, we will assume that this is true and say that the real part of the complex number is a. For example the real part of 2+3i is 2 and we write this by saying ℜ(2+3i) = 2. Correspondingly the imaginary part is ℑ(2+3i) = 3.

Although i = sqrt(_1) is our definition, this isn't actually the most useful fact about i. We will much more often use the fact that i^2 = _1 which is an immediate consequence of the definition.

Question: How do you operate on complex numbers?

Answer: Obviously (a+ib) + (c+id) = (a+c) + i(b+d) so that addition goes pieces-wise. For instance (3-2i) + (_4-i) = _1-3i.

It's not a lot harder to imagine how multiplication goes. (a+ib)(c+id) = ac + iad + ibc + i^2bd and from here we can factor the i on the middle terms. And of course we can also use the important feature that i^2 = _1. This simplifies the expression to ac + i(ad+bc) - bd. Typically we group the real and imaginary parts so that we prefer to write this as (a+ib)(c+id) = ac-bd + i(ad+bc). To illustrate the use of this, (1+2i)(3-4i) = (1*3-(2*_4)) + i(1*_4 + 2*3) = _5 + i10.

Much harder is to figure out how to divide complex numbers. What can we do to `\frac{a+ib}{c+id}`? What do we even want out of this? As usual we want this written in standard form, that is to say, we want to identify the real and imaginary parts. To get that we introduce the notion of the conjugate. In particular we will use the conjugate of c+id. The conjugate of c+id we denote by writing a bar over the expression, `\overline{c+id}` and this is defined to be c-id. That's right, all you do is flip the + to -! Easy as that. If it were already a - then flip it to a +. What is the point of the conjugate? It has the property that if you multiply (c+id)(c-id) you will have no complex part! (c+id)(c-id) = c^2+d^2. Make sure you can confirm this equality on your own. The reason this matters will become apparent soon.

Let's take the example `\frac{4-3i}{_2-i}`. To simplify we compute

$$\frac{4-3i}{\_2-i}\ast\frac{\_2+i}{\_2+i} = \frac{(4-3i)(\_2+i)}{4+1} = \\\\ \frac{\_8+3+i(4+6)}{5} = \frac{\_5+i10}{5} \\\\ \_1+i2$$

The fact that these numbers simplified so nicely to integer real and imaginary parts was a lucky accident. Often the result of these computations look like, for instance `\frac{7}{3}-i\frac{2}{5}`. Still we obtain real and imaginary parts from this, which is what we wanted.

Question: Is i < 1?

Answer: This is a curious question. We have given no ordering relation on the complex numbers. We could pick one to enforce, but which one would be natural? Should we order them by the real part? The imaginary part? Some combination thereof?

We've been thinking about i in a mostly arithmetic and partly algebraic way. We'll discuss this more later, but one extremely important idea is that they also have a geometric interpretation. What could that even mean? The real numbers were clear, because they filled the gaps in the number line, but that was everything we needed wasn't it? Wasn't it?

It turns out that putting the complex numbers on the coordinate plane, with the real numbers running horizontally and the imaginary numbers running vertically, is a really good idea.

__Example__

Here we have illustrated the location of 2+i and _2-i and 2-i. We are effectively putting the real and imaginary point in coordinates on a normal coordinate plane. This is called the complex plane.

In a sense this is telling us something about how we're choosing to order the complex numbers. It ain't using the real part or the imaginary part, neither one seems privileged in this picture. If anything we would naturally order them by how close they are to the origin, or 0, of the plane. Thus 0 has distance 0, 1 has distance 1, i also has distance 1, and so on. We won't worry right now about the distance of a point like 3+4i, it's enough just to know that the notion of the distance is well-defined and that this is how we're ordering the numbers. To indicate the distance of a number from 0 we write |.|. Thus |i| = 1 and |1| = 1 and |_1| = 1 and |i10| = 10 and so on. Notice that this agrees with the previous definition of |.| as meaning the absolute value, which only removed the negative if it existed. In fact we often call this the absolute value of the complex number, although I prefer to call it the magnitude.

An unfortunate consequence of that is that clearly, the ordering does not uniquely sort the numbers. By that I mean to indicate that 1 and i are equally distant from 0 and therefore we cannot pick out numbers just based on their distance. That also wasn't true of the reals since 1 and _1 are also equally distant from 0, but at least on the reals every two numbers can be compared with < and that's not true with complex numbers.

However, we can still identify numbers by picking and distance and then an angle.

__Example__

The number 1 has distance 1 and angle 0. The angle is always measured by rotating counter-clockwise from the positive real axis. The number i has angle 90o. Here I am using degree-measure, and assuming that it's common knowledge that a full circular rotation is indicated by 360o. We call the angle of a complex number its argument, and write Arg(1) = 0o. By this system of identifying numbers, Arg(_1) = 90o and Arg(_i) = 270o. It's intuitively clear that Arg(1+i) = 45o. Notice that if you specify a magnitude and argument, there is exactly one complex number located at that point in the complex plane.

In a sense, this can now serve as our sorting of the numbers, so that complex numbers w < x is true if either |w| < |x| or, if |w| =|x| then Arg(w) < Arg(x). We typically don't actually do this--typically we just don't care about ordering the complex numbers because at this point it's kind of useless--but we could if we wanted. Still, the notions of complex plane, magnitude, and argument are useful.

Question: What is the relationship between the operations on complex numbers, and their position in the complex plane?

Answer: Of course summing complex numbers just acts like a shift. If you take a+ib and add c+id you basically just move it right by c and up by d.

__Example__

What about multiplication? Let's see what happens when we multiply (3+i4)(1+i).

__Example__

No clear pattern stands out, except that it gets farther away from 0 than either of the original numbers were to begin with, and it's farther to the left than they are. Let's do it again with easier numbers. Let's look at 2i*_3.

__Example__

Again farther away from 0. The distances were 2 and 3 and now it's 6. Looks like the product of distances. This is right, and we'll prove it later. What about the argument? It was 90o and 180o respectively, now it's 270o. That looks like the sum. Again this is right and we'll prove it later. If we look back at the first example, although we may not be able to say exactly what the distances are, it looks approximately right that |(3+i4)(1+i)| = |3+i4| * |1+i| which is to say the distance of the result is the product of the two individual distances. It also looks right that Arg( (3+i4)(1+i) ) = Arg(3+i4) + Arg(1+i) which is to say that the angle of the result is the sum of the two individual angles.

This is really quite amazing: We get genuinely meaningful geometric transformations from doing genuinely meaningful arithmetic operations. Addition provides the geometric operation of translation; multiplication simultaneously causes "dilation" (scaling toward or away from 0) and rotation.

Exercise: We've seen the use of multiplication to dilate (scale) points away from 0. What has to be true so that we dilate a point toward 0? Also, we've seen how to rotate a point counter-clockwise. How might you rotate it clockwise?

Answer: If you multiply by a complex number with distance less than 1, it will dilate numbers toward 0. To rotate counter-clockwise, you can divide rather than multiply. This makes some intuitive sense and we'll leave it at that. Notice that division by a number with magnitude greater than 1 shrinks the magnitude of the quotient while division by a number of magnitude less than 1 grows the magnitude of the quotient.

Note: It turns out that the complex numbers are "algebraically complete" and therefore we will never need a bigger number set than this for standard algebraic purposes. Hence we end the development here. However, in the next chapter, we will backtrack and re-develop some of these things in alternative constructions. In any case, let's close this chapter by defining the notion of a set's subset. We have already said what set membership means. This tells us when a single thing is "in" a set. Now we will say what it means for a whole set to be "in" another set. For illustration purposes we will use set A = {1, 2, 3} and B = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7} and C = {4, 9}. Because every element in A is also found in B we say that A is a subset of B. To express this we write A ⊆ B. Notice that no other subset relation holds among the three sets. So A ⊈ C and B ⊈ C and C ⊈ B and so on.
