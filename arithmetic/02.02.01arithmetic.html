Arithmetic

Chapter 02 - Extending number systems

Section 02 - Rationals

Abstract: Rationals are the closure under division and represent the ability to segment a quantity into any number of pieces of equal size--they also represent ratios.

Terms: Rational, reciprocal, decimal expansion

Symbols: ℚ, ∊, .

Question: What do we get if we next close our number system under division as we did with subtraction?

Answer: We form the set ℚ = {z : z = x/y where x,y are integers and y is not 0}. In fact, let's introduce another piece of notation for sets, so that we can economize expressions a little more. We use the symbol ∊ to mean "in" so that x∊A claims that element x is in set A. This let's us say ℚ = {z : z = x/y, for x,y ∊ ℤ and y ≠ 0}. Of course that's the abstract answer. Physically this corresponds to endlessly breaking quantities into pieces even below the level of the single unit. For instance 2/3 is physically interpreted as breaking a unit into three equal pieces and keeping two. We call these numbers rational numbers because they correspond to the concept of a ratio of two numbers. We call the set ℚ for "quotient" since in a sense, it is the set of all quotients of integers.

Note: It is a little ironic that rationals come after the integers in the standard construction of number sets, and yet rationals were "accepted" as numbers earlier than the integers were. The Greeks were relatively satisfied with the idea of dividing a quantity, if not infinitely, then at least very much. However, they rejected any notion of a negative since these numbers cannot do any counting--so they thought anyway.

Question: So we have rationals now. How do we add, multiply, and so on?

Answer: This is a pretty tough one to answer a priori. Most readers will undoubtedly know the routine but perhaps not the justifications, even if they have a fairly good Math background. To sum any (p/q) + (r/s) is too difficult to answer directly. For instance what is (1/2) + (1/3)? Let's focus first on an easier question.

What's (1/3) + (1/3)? Of course in each case we have a quantity obtained by dividing a unit into three equal pieces and keeping one of them. If we sum them, we now have two of them. So (1/3) + (1/3) = 2/3. In fact, from this perspective, it might be helpful to think of the bottom number not as dividing but as measuring in a certain standard length. For instance we could regard 1 as meaning 1 meter, and then 1/3 just means that the unit of measurement is thirds-of-a-meter and there's one of them. From this perspective, summing (1/2) + (1/3) is trying to sum lengths measured in different units of measurement. To figure out the sum we need to convert units, but we need to find a "bridge" unit that we can convert both of them into.

With a bit of thought you might realize that by breaking halves into three equal pieces, you get six equally sized pieces; and by breaking thirds into two equally sized pieces you get six equally sized pieces. So 1/2 can be converted into 3/6 and 1/3 can be converted into 2/6, so that sixths is our bridge-unit. From here we can sum (1/2) + (1/3) = (3/6) + (2/6) = 5/6.

The standard high school algorithm is to get a "common denominator". Often this means just multiplying the two denominators, like 2*3 = 6 as in the previous example. However, in some cases it is possible to find a different bridge unit. Take for instance (7/4) + (11/6). We could choose 24 as the bridge unit (or really 24ths, but you know what I mean) but 12 is possible too and easier to use. (7/4) + (11/6) = (21/12) + (22/12) = 43/12.

Question: How do we find the smallest possible bridge unit?

Answer: It's the lcm of the two denominators! Of course this makes sense, we need to give each denominator just enough extra factors until they contain the same factors and so are the same number--but we don't want to give any more factors than we have to. This is just what the lcm does.

For instance, lcm(2,3) = 6 and lcm(4,6) = 12. The lcm in a sense gives 2 a factor of 3 and gives 3 a factor of 2, in order to convert both into 6. The work that we put into understanding primes and factorization is coming in handy.

Question: How do we subtract rationals?

Answer: Of course the same method applies, in which we get a "common denominator" and compute the difference of the numerators.

Question: How do we multiply rationals?

Answer: Again this is hard to approach directly. Choosing an easier intermediate question, what if the right factor is an integer? We use repeated addition or some similar trick as we studied in the previous section--and we require multiplication to be commutative so that this works also if there's an integer in the left factor. This then means that p/q * r = pr/q.

If we are to next try to understand (p/q) * (r/s) then it would be helpful to think of this as actually saying ((p/q)*r)/s. No, I'm not using associativity here, as that's not valid for division--instead, I'm interpreting "* (r/s)" as "multiply by r and then divide by s". In a sense I'm packing a special parenthesis-shifting law into the very definition of multiplying rational numbers. Notice that we saw in the previous chapter that natural numbers have this property assuming that q|p and s|r, so in a sense we're just enforcing that rationals also have this property even when divisibility does not hold.

But to seal the deal we need to say how one divides a rational by an integer. What should (p/q) / z be? Of course if we break the units into q equal pieces and then break each of those into z equal pieces, then we have qz equal pieces. Thus (p/q) / z should be the same as p / (qz).

At this point we're justified in saying that (p/q) * (r/s) = (pr) / (qs). To reiterate, the logic is that we construe this as first multiplication by r putting it in the numerator, then division by s putting it in the denominator. So for instance (1/2) (3/4) = 3/8.

Exercise: Justify why, if p and q are integers with any common factor, call it m, then p/q is the same as the rational number with the m removed from both p and q. So for instance 2 and 10 have a common factor of 2, therefore 2/10 = 1/5.

Answer: We can give a physical and an abstract answer. The abstract answer is that, if p, q share factor m then there exist integers x, y such that p = mx, q = my and therefore p/q = (mx)/(my) = (m/m)(x/y) = 1(x/y) = x/y. Of course the fact that m/m = 1 is something we knew from natural number operations.

The physical answer is that m only breaks up quantities in the denominator, and then puts just as many back together in the numerator. Think of 2/4 which has a factor of 2 in both the numerator and denominator. This means that we broke a unit in half and then in half again, and took two of the halves. We could have just as well broken the unit in half and stopped.

Question: It's harder to compare rationals than it is to compare integers. Which is bigger, 81/37 or 100/51? There are ad hoc tricks that you can leverage to answer this. But let's make a general procedure once and for all. If p/q and r/s are rational numbers, give an algorithm that assumes only the ability to compare integers, in order to determine which is larger.

Answer: One way to do this is to assume p/q < r/s in order to investigate what is equivalent to this being true. Intuitively we should be able to multiply both sides of this by any number we want--as long as we pick the same number on both sides. I mean, think of how it would work with integers. Since 20 < 30 then by doubling we get 40 < 60, and we could even halve and get 10 < 15. The numbers may grow farther apart or closer together but they never switch sides. You can even think of this process as "scaling things up" or scaling down. Of course this assumes that we multiply or divide by a positive. If you experiment with a negative then things don't work so well, so to keep things easy let's assume s is positive.

Anyway, with that principle established then p/q < r/s is equivalent to s(p/q) < sr/s which can simplify to sp/q < r. Does this look like improvement? Well at least we have only one fraction rather than two. If we do this again with q this time, again assuming q is positive, then we get the expression ps < rq which is equivalent to the one we started with after doing all appropriate cancellation. This serves as our test, or a very short algorithm: p/q is less than r/s if ps is less than qr.

A natural question is, what happens if one multiplies an inequation like this by a negative? Experimentation will show you that it always flips the inequation. Can we prove that it does? Suppose p < q where p, q may be integers or rationals. Then simply by subtraction on both sides by p and q we see that _q < _p. But this also has the upshot that this is the result of multiplying by _1. Hence multiplying by _1 flips the inequation around. Multiplying by any other negative quantity is the same as multiplying by _1 and then the "rest" of the number so to speak, so that this proof really covers all cases. We have shown, then, that multiplying by any negative flips the inequation.

As a closing note before we move on to other questions, of course a test for less-than is automatically a reverse test for greater-than. Testing whether 3/7 > 4/9 is just a matter of testing whether 4/9 < 3/7 so we already have that covered. A test for equality can work exactly the same way, although a possibly more efficient algorithm is simply to reduce the fraction to its simplest expression--and it makes some obvious sense to believe that every rational number has a reduction to a least expression. We'll discuss that idea more in Number Theory but for now it just makes good sense.

Question: How do we divide rationals?

Answer: As usual (p/q) / (r/s) means finding the number which when multiplied by r/s results in p/q. Of course an easy number that has this property is (p/q)*(s/r) because when you multiply this by r/s you get (p/q)(s/r)(r/s) = (p/q)(rs/rs).

Note: Now that operations are getting big and ugly, I'm not going to be a stickler about the left-associativity rule that I asserted earlier. To be more precise, I'll use left-associativity on any operations that are written in infix notation, but not if I use any other notation like concatenation. I'll also take to writing fractions as `\frac{rs}{rs}` and pay no attention to the left-associativity rule in such cases.

In any case what we've just found is the so-called "flip and multiply" rule for dividing rationals: `\frac{p // q}{r // s} = \frac{p}{q}\ast \frac{s}{r}`.

Because the "flip" operation is useful for precisely this reason then we give it the name of the reciprocal. Thus the "flip and multiply" rule is inadequate, since most students confused this at some time in their lives for thinking that `\frac{p//q}{r//s} = \frac{q}{p}\ast\frac{r}{s}`. Instead the rule should be something like "fix-product-flip" to indicate that the numerator remains fixed and only the denominator is flipped.

Question: How do we exponentiate?

Answer: We skipped this question when the power was an integer last time, and now we return to that case. Recall that we skipped this because 1/2 had no meaning for us in that section but it does now. By that sort of justification we can claim that 2^_1 = 1/2 and 2^_2 should be half as big and therefore should be 1/4, and so on. This justifies claiming that, if n is any natural number then 2^_n = 1/2^n.

We could justify it another way. Notice that we said x^px^q = x^p+q because we are aggregating the factors of x. By a similar logic, so long as the resulting power is a natural number, we can justify saying `\frac{x^{\wedge p}}{x^{\wedge q}} = x^{\wedge p-q}` because in the fraction we are canceling factors of x. But if we try to apply this for, say, `\frac{2^{\wedge 1}}{2^{\wedge 2}} = \frac{1}{2} = 2^{\wedge 1-2} = 2^{\wedge \_1}` then we get the same result as we did from the previous logic, making this sound like a consistently good answer.

The but immediately the question becomes harder when we try to figure out the exponential where the power is a rational as well. What should 2^1/2 be? One indication can again come from our exponential laws, since if we raise a power to a power, i.e. find `(2^{\wedge 1/2})^{\wedge 2}` we should be able to multiply the powers and get 2^1 which is 2. That is to say, if we square this quantity we get 2. But that's exactly what the square-root is! Thus $$x^{\wedge 1/n} = \sqrt[n]{x}$$ and more generally $$x^{\wedge m/n} = \sqrt[n]{x^m}$$

Exercise: Find `8^{\wedge 2/3}` and `2^{\wedge 1/2}`.

Answer: The former is easiest to compute by first finding the cube-root, which is 2, then squaring which is 4. Of course the official rule about this is to do things in reverse order, but it's easy enough to show that the order doesn't matter, you'll get the same answer either way.

The latter does not exist in our number system.

Question: Can we prove that sqrt(2) is not a rational?

Answer: This is a very famous proof. We do this by contradiction, which means that we assume sqrt(2) is rational and then prove that a contradiction follows; therefore the assumption must have been false.

If sqrt(2) is rational then there must be integers p, q such that they share no common factor and p/q = sqrt(2). In that case 2 = p^2/q^2 and from that we can infer that 2q^2 = p^2. This shows that p^2 has a factor of 2 and must be even. But that factor of 2 must have come from p and therefore p^2 must have two factors of 2. Well in that case 2q^2 must also have two factors of 2. One of them is exhibited clearly but the other one must come from q^2. But in that case q has a factor of 2! But we started off by asserting that p and q had no common factors and now they share 2! ⚡

Note: the ⚡ symbol is used to denote that we have arrived at a contradiction, and is used to indicate the end of a proof by contradiction.

Exercise: Prove that cbrt(2) and sqrt(3) are not rational.

Answer: The same proof applies but either taking a third power (former) or taking a second power and showing they share a factor of 3 (latter).

Question: Since sqrt(2) is not rational, what do we say about it?

Answer: For this section we say that it is not a rational number, but we can approximate it with rational numbers. That is to say, we can use 1 as a first approximation since 1^2 = 1 is too small. As a next approximation we could try 3/2 but its square, 9/4, is larger than 2 and therefore over-shoots the goal. We could test 4/3 and find that this is too small but only by a little. If we continue the process of guessing we can find numbers which square to something closer and closer to 2, although the proof above demonstrates that we will never find it perfectly.

Question: We have been describing all rational numbers as quotients, but it also makes some sense to find points in-between integers by writing an expression like 2.3 to mean a number that is between 2 and 3 and closer to 2 than 3. How much closer? If you broke up the distance from 2 to 3 into ten equal pieces, we could label them 2.1, 2.2, ..., 2.9. So 2.3 is the number that is 3 tenths of the way from 2 to 3.

But given that this is a digit expansion, we want to be able to express it as coefficients of powers of 10. That is to say, since we regard 456 as 4*10^2 + 5*10^1 + 6*10^0 then how should we choose to represent 2.3 in order to continue the pattern?

Answer: This is called the decimal expansion of a number, in which we represent say 3/2 as 1.5 or as 1*10^0 + 5*10^_1. That is to say, digit positions to the right of the decimal mark those digits that are coefficients of a negative power of 10. This coheres with what we've said elsewhere about negative powers, which is that 1*10^0 + 5*10^_1 = 1 + 5/10 = 3/2. In this way every decimal expansion that terminates at some power of 10 has a representation as a ratio. Is the reverse true?

Exercise: Convert 1.21 into a ratio. Also convert 1/3 into a decimal expansion.

Answer: 1.21 = 121/100

Converting 1/3 is substantially harder. We want an equivalent expression that will tell us what the coefficient of 1/10 should be (obviously this number is smaller than 1 and so has no units digit). Clearly 3 is the largest coefficient we can give it before the number becomes too large, so that 1/3 is approximated by 3/10 but there is still a bit left-over. The left-over bit is 1/3 - 3/10 = 1/30. We now need to find an expression for this in decimal form and add it to our current best approximation. The same problem as before arises, so we approximate 1/30 by 3/100, which has the largest coefficient of 1/100 that we could pick before the number grows too large.

Of course this process repeats itself so that 1/3 ≈ 3/10 + 3/100 + 3/1000 = 0.333 and we can keep getting better approximations of 1/3 by choosing to expand more digits. I write ≈ to mean "approximately equal to" which, for now, is a subjective statement. I don't want to write 1/3 = 0.333... just yet because, although the equality is true, we can only claim this when we have adequate justification for the "..." and that only comes once we have studied Real Analysis. In any case, for now, take the statement that 1/3 ≈ 0.333... to mean that every extra digit of 0.333... gets you closer and closer to 1/3.

Let's do it again for 1/7, but this time with a twist. Let's assume that in some sense we already have the coefficients and therefore we can write

$$\frac{1}{7} \approx \frac{a_1}{10} + \frac{a_2}{100} + \frac{a_3}{100} + ...$$

If we multiply both sides by 7 then we have

$$1 \approx \frac{7a_1}{10} + \frac{7a_2}{100} + \frac{7a_3}{1000} + ...$$

What this tells us that `a_1`, the coefficient for 1/10, must be the largest multiple of 7 not to exceed 10. If `7a_1` were to exceed 10 we'd have a number bigger than 1 which would be a contradiction. Then for `a_2` we have to pick the largest multiple not to exceed the remainder from the previous choice--and so on. This hints at an algorithm for computing the decimal expansion.

Exercise: Find the ratio that is approximated by .1234123412341234...

Answer: This won't be a thoroughly rigorously justified method just yet, but it should make some sense. Call x = .123412341234... Then if we multiply by 10000 this will shift the decimal four places so that 10000x = 1234.12341234... Then we can cancel the decimals trailing off to infinity, so to speak, by finding 10000x - x = 1234.12341234... - 0.12341234... = 1234. This was computing the decimal expansion difference but if we also factor the left-hand-side we get (10000-1)x which is 9999x. This tells us that 9999x = 1243 and so x = 1234/9999.

This wasn't a rigorous construction because it subtracted an infinity, so to speak, of digits from another infinity of digits. We'll work on tightening up the justification later but for now, just notice that if you compute the decimal expansion of 1234/9999, you get .12341243... so this method does seem to be getting it right even if we don't have a rigorous proof of correctness.

Question: Does every decimal expansion correspond to a rational number?

Answer: We found an algorithm, so to speak, that finds a decimal expansions that keeps getting close to sqrt(2) even though we know that this number doesn't exist in the rationals. It was a guess-and-check algorithm, but it keeps finding new rational numbers that are better and better approximations of sqrt(2) without end. In running this algorithm we find that it's 1.41421356... If we consider the decimal sequence which has "all" of the decimals that approximate sqrt(2), then this must not correspond to any rational number.

Here's another decimal sequence that is not a rational number: 0.1011011101111011111...

Question: Can we prove that every digit expansion with a non-repeating sequence of digits must not be rational? To say the same thing in reverse: Can we prove that every rational number produces a repeating digit expansion? Hint: if we use the division algorithm on two integers, and the algorithm keeps running to infinity, but the remainder at each step is always less than the quotient, what could this tell us?

Answer: Since the remainder at every step is less than the quotient, there are a finite number of possible values of the remainder. However, if we assume we are generating an infinity of digits by this process, then at some point the remainder must come up again in the process. Once we arrive at a remainder that we've seen before, the sequence of remainders after it and therefore the digit sequence, will have to repeat over and over.

The upshot of these considerations are i) that we now have a proof of the claim above that 0.10110111... is irrational, ii) that the rational numbers are exactly the same numbers as those with repeating digit sequences, and iii) therefore not every digit sequence corresponds to a rational number.

Exercise: Give a general characterization of what happens when you divide a number, say x, by a positive number bigger than 1. Also characterize what happens when you divide by 1. Also characterize what happens when you divide by a positive number less than 1. Also characterize what happens when you divide by a negative.

Answer: Of course when dividing x by a positive number greater than 1, it shrinks toward 0. If x is positive that means it gets smaller, like going from 10 and then after dividing by 2 becoming 5. If x is negative like _10, that means getting bigger by becoming _5. Of course dividing by 1 doesn't change the number. By dividing by a positive number less than 1, like 1/2, this makes x "dilate" away from 0. For instance `\frac{10}{1//2} = \frac{10}{1}\ast \frac{2}{1} = 20`. A similar calculation holds for negative x.

If you divide by a negative, same principles described above hold, after you first flip x to the other side of 0.
