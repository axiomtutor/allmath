Arithmetic

Chapter 03: Counting

Section 02: p-adic numbers

Abstract: p-adic numbers encode information about divisibility by powers of primes, therefore motivating the definition of two p-adic numbers being close in terms of divisibility by p. This in turn motivates how we construct subtraction and division. This still extends to a bigger class of numbers by closing it under division.

Question: One tantalizing way to introduce the idea of p-adic integers is to ask, given all of the decisions that we've made in constructing these number sets, could we have made other decisions to get other equally good or better number systems?

Answer: Although the question is too open-ended to answer completely, there is at least one affirmative answer. We construct the natural numbers as usual, represented in some base system. Let's go ahead and use decimal numbers. However, rather than close our number system under subtraction in the way that we did in chapter 02, we instead think of the following: When we add 1 and 9 to get 10, a 0 pops up. If we add 1 and 99 two 0's pop up. Of course they're lead by a 1 in front but ... What if we say that ...9999 is a number. A number with infinitely many digits containing 9. If we then add it to 1, every single digit will be 0. In this way we could call ...999 the negative of 1.

In a similar way, we can understand multiplication. Again rather than closing our number system under an operation, we can think of 1/3 for example as that number which when multiplied by 3 results in 1. ...6667 will have this property. Unfortunately it turns out that, for instance, 1/2 does not have such a number. We cannot multiply 2 with anything that will leave a digit of 1 in the ones place and therefore this cannot have a reciprocal. The same sort of thing is true for 5. It's pretty captivating to think that this observation must somehow be relevant to the fact that, by inspecting the ones-digit of any number, you can immediately tell whether it's even (the ones-digit will have to be even if it is) or if its divisible by 5 (the ones-digit will have to be congruent to 0 mod 5 if it is). These two numbers are special in base-10 precisely because they are the factors of 10.

In base-2 or in any base that is a prime, the only numbers that don't have a reciprocal are of the form p^n. Hence when studying p-adic numbers we require that the base, p, be a prime number.

In fact modern computers use approximately this sort of structure to encode negative numbers. Of course computers are finite machines so they restrict to a finite sequence of digits, and the left-most digit is regarded as, in some sense the "infinite digit place".

Question: Although that's a fun thought, it may not be the best motivation for studying p-adic numbers. The study of p-adic integers was motivated by interest in using the number's digit representation to learn about divisibility. It's interesting just how easy it is to determine divisibility by choosing representation in a base. In base 10 if a number ends in 0 then it's clearly divisible by 10 and therefore divisible by both 2 and 5. If it ends in more 0's it's therefore divisible by powers of 10 and therefore powers of 2 and 5. It has the unfortunate feature that, if it ends in say three 0's it may be divisible by more than three powers of 2. Take for instance 800 which is divisible by 2^6. Hence the divisibility information is not "pure" in a sense because the base is not a prime.

In binary numbers we can read the divisibility by 2 in a more pure way: A (non-zero) number ends in (at least) three 0's if and only if it is divisible by 2^3. The same sort of purity is available in any other prime base.

There seems to be a relationship between a number's remainder and the digit representation of that number. So the question is, what is that relation? Represent 5 in base-3 and also compute 5 mod 3. What do you notice, and does this work for other examples?

Answer: 5 in base-3 is 12. 5 mod 3 is 2. Of course the interesting observation is the matching 2's. If we picked 22 it would have base-3 representation of 2*3^2 + 1*3^1 + 1*3^0 = 211. Here again 22 mod 3 is 1 so that the ones-digit matches the mod. Of course that's trivially always going to work because we've learned before that we can either mod after summing or before summing.

2*3^2 + 1*3^1 + 1*3^0 mod 3 = 0 + 0 + 1

Question: Can we extend this discovery to other digits?

Answer: If previously we found the 3^0 digit by modding by 3, we can investigate the 3^1 digit by modding by 3^2.

2*3^2 + 1*3^1 + 1*3^0 mod 9 = 1*3^1 + 1*3^0

If we want to learn just about the "threes-digit", we'd need to subtract off the 1. Then it would show that the threes-digit comes from 1*3^1, or said more simply, the digit is 1. In a sense the mod operator is an annihilator for any terms that have a factor matching the mod number.

If we mod by 3^3 to try to learn the "nines-digit" it is easily computed, because 22 is smaller than the mod number 27, so the remainder is trivially 22. Also, no digit mods to 0 because none of them have a base of 3^3 or larger. Hence

2*3^2 + 1*3^1 + 1*3^0 mod 27 = 2*3^2 + 1*3^1 + 1*3^0

and if we then subtract off everything else that we've found so far, the 1*3^1 + 1*3^0, all that remains is the 2*3^2 and we have our answer.

A lot of the ease here was due to the fact that we already knew the base-3 representation of 22. If we pick another random number like 77 and repeat the process, this time using the process to find the base-3 representation, we find

77 mod 3 = 2 so this is the units-digit.

77 mod 9 = 5 so the threes-digit is found from 5-2 = 3 = 1*3^1.

77 mod 27 = 23 so the nines-digit is from 23 - (3+2) = 18 = 2*3^2.

77 mod 81 = 77 so the twenty-sevens-digit is from 77 - (18+3+2) = 54 = 2*3^3.

From this we have found that the base-3 representation of 77 is 2212. We can confirm the answer by computing 2*3^3 + 2*3^2 + 1*3^1 + 2*3^0.

Exercise: Find 1234 in base 5 using the process above.

Answer: Commas are inserted below to separate calculations:

1234 mod 5 = 4 , , , 1234 mod 25 = 9 , , , 1234 mod 125 = 109 , , , 1234 mod 625 = 609 , , , 1234 mod 3125 = 1234

14 - 9 = 5 = 1*5^1 , , , 109 - 9 = 100 = 4*5^2 , , , 609 - 109 = 500 = 4*5^3 , , , 1234 - 609 = 625 = 1*5^4

The representation is 14414.

Notice that if we had kept taking mods with higher powers of 5 then we would only repeatedly see 1234.

1234 mod 5^5 = 1234 , , , 1234 mod 5^6 = 1234 and so on.

Hence every digit thereafter is 1234-1234 = 0.

Question: What if we represent a negative number in a base system, using the same sort of logic? What is -30 in base-7?

Answer:

-30 mod 7 = 5 , , , -30 mod 49 = 19 , , , -30 mod 343 = 313 , , , -30 mod 2401 = 2371 ...

19 - 5 = 14 = 2*7^1 , , , 313 - 19 = 294 = 6*7^2 , , , 2371 - 313 = 6*7^3 ...

So far we have the digits ...6625. If we continue computing further digits they will always be 6. Why? The simplest explanation may be to see what happens when we sum this with 30. 30 has representation 42 in base-7. Therefore we sum

Because this is an infinite digit we shouldn't really call this base-7. Instead we call it 7-adic. Since ...66625 + 30 = 0 therefore ...66625 is the negative of 30. It will actually turn out that every strictly negative 7-adic number will have infinitely many 6's toward the left. Every strictly negative 5-adic number has infinitely many 4's to the left; in base-p the strictly negative numbers have infinitely many (p-1)'s to the left. I say "strictly negative" rather than just "negative" for reasons that will become clear.

Question: By using p-adic integers we easily inspect how many times an integer is divisible by p. If its first n digits are 0 then it can be divided by p^n. This fact works even when n = 0 and in a sense even works when n = ∞. When n = 0 the integer is "not very divisible by p" and when n = 1billion it is very divisible by p, and when n = ∞ the number is 0 and is therefore as divisible by p as it is possible to have. Because this is one of the things that makes p-adic integers most interesting, this is the metric by which we decide how "big" an integer is. We define |x|, the p-adic absolute value or magnitude of x, to be the largest p^n such that p^n | x. Because this is different for different values of p we in fact write it as |x|p. For instance |1|7 = 0 and |2|7 = 0, but |7|7 = 7 and |14|7 = 7, and |49|7 = 2. With this established it makes some sense to say that 0 is the largest integer in this system, as it can be divided infinitely by p.

We have said what the size of an integer is in this system. We often define the distance between some two points as the absolute value of the difference. What is the distance between 7 and 14 in 7-adic numbers? What is the distance between 7 and 49?

Answer: |14-7|7 = |7|7 = 7 and |49-7|7 = |42|7 = 7.

Question: What can we do about the fact that not every number has a reciprocal? We've seen that 1/7 does not exist in p-adic integers, nor does 1/49.

Answer: We can use the old closure trick. Since 1/7 doesn't exist, we just close you system under the division operation. The set of p-adic integers closed under division (by non-zero integers) is called the p-adic rationals and we denote this by Qp. These are a bit unwieldy however, so I won't discuss them more now. In order to focus on things of a computational nature, I'll bring up one last point that hopefully sells p-adic numbers as genuinely interesting rather than a kooky but pointless play thing.

Question: What are the solutions of x^2 ≡3 25? What about mod 9 and 27?

Answer: We've seen solutions to linear congruence equations, now we have a quadratic. It's no surprise that 5 is a solution and it shouldn't be much more of a surprise that _5 is too. The representation of 5 in base-3 is not terribly illuminating but _5 has the following modules from which we could investigate its representation.

_5 ≡3 1

_5 ≡9 4

_5 ≡27 22

_5 ≡81 76

and provocatively, 22 is a solution to x^2 ≡9 25 and 76 is a solution to x^2 ≡27 25 and so on.

Exercise: Discover a similar pattern in the solutions of x^2 ≡5 49. This provocative cliffhanger will be the end of our study until we circle back around to it when studying Number Theory.

Answer: The trivial solution is 7. The modules of _7 are

_7 ≡5 3

_7 ≡25 18

_7 ≡125 118

and a solution to x^2 ≡25 49 is 18, and so on.
