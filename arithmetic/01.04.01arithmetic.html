Section 4: Exponentiation, root, and logarithm

Abstract: Exponentiation is repeated multiplication and it has two inverses, the root and the logarithm.

Terms: Exponentiation, exponential, base, power, square-root, cube-root, root, logarithm, exponential representation, scientific notation.

Symbols: ^, sqrt, cbrt, ln, log

Question: What's the next natural step after multiplication?

Answer: Exponentiation which is repeated multiplication, just like multiplication is repeated addition.

To establish the vocabulary, if x is multiplied with itself y times, we write x^y and call this the exponential. We call x the base and y the power. I've decided to use both the super-script notation in combination with the ^ notation, even though typically we only pick one or the other. One reason is that eventually I'll want to use superscripts for indices and the inclusion of the ^ will help. Also ^ is common but notice that it is not the correct symbol in Python. Instead Python has reserved ^ to mean "exclusive or", something we'll come to in the section on Logic. You of course are free to play with the ^ operation, it's a free country and kind of a free internet but ANYWAY. Our current topic is exponentiation and as we've seen elsewhere this is performed with the ** notation in Python. This is (hopefully) the only point on which my notation will substantially diverge from Python's.

Question: Why should we care about exponentiation? Doesn't this typically just describe the area of a square (for a power of 2) and volume of a cube (for a power of 3)? Since there is no fourth dimension (something the ancient Greeks thought, and therefore disregarded powers greater than 3) isn't a larger power meaningless and therefore we could just have special words and symbols for second and third powers?

Answer: We do use the word "squared" to mean a second power, so that 4 squared is 16, and we use "cubed" to mean a third power so that 4 cubed is 64. However, there has been a fourth dimension ever since Einstein, and really there's been one ever since ancient philosophers thought about time as an extra dimension, and really there have been more than 4 dimensions since the Big Bang. Let's just allow ourselves any powers that we want, in case we ever need them.

But also, powers are an excellent way to describe base systems! Take the number 54321. This is the same as (5*10^4) + (4*10^3)+ (3*102) + (2*10) + 1. In fact, this pattern could partially justify us asserting that 10^1 = 10 and 10^0 = 1 so that we can fill the pattern more fully:

54321 = (5*10^4) + (4*10^3) + (3*102) + (2*10^1) + (1*10^0)

I'm not sure if this representation of a number has a name but I'll choose to call this the exponential representation of a number. We call 5 the coefficient of 10^4 and likewise for the other digits.

Exercise: If x = (a*10^2) + (2*10^1) + (3*10^0) and y = (4*10^1) + (9*10^0) and x+y = (a*10^2) + (a*10^1) + (2*10^0) then what is a?

Answer: This is something between a Math and Logic puzzle. Since the ones-digits sum and create a digit that must carry to the tens, then in the tens place we can infer that 1+2+4 = a so that 7 = a. The rest of the information in the setup wasn't even necessary.

Question: Does exponentiation have any of the properties we've studied before, like commutativity, associativity, or distributivity?

Answer: Easy examples confirm it's neither associative nor commutative.

$$2^{\wedge3} = 8, \quad 3^{\wedge2} = 9$$

$$2^{\land 2^{\land 3}} = 2^{\land 8} = 256, \quad (2^{\land 2})^{\land 3} = 4^{\land 3} = 64$$

Distributive is more interesting because, as we saw, there is no such thing as distributive per se, but only distributive over this or that operation. We can easily show that exponentiation is not distributive over addition.

$$(1+2)^{\land 2} = 9,\quad 1^{\land 2} + 2^{\land 2} = 5$$

However, it seems to be distributive over multiplication: $$(2\ast 3)^{\land 2} = 6^{\land 2} = 36, \quad 2^{\land 2}\ast 3^{\land 2} = 4\ast 9 = 36$$

Can we prove that this is true for all numbers?

Before jumping to that, let's also declare formally that we will again assume right-associativity with all operations, including exponentiation. Thus `2^{\wedge 3^{\wedge 4}}` is assumed to mean `2^{\wedge (3^{\wedge 4})}`.

Exercise: Prove that (xy)^n = x^ny^n. Hint: Write (xy)^n as repeated multiplication and use the associativity and commutativity of multiplication to re-group factors.

Answer:

$$(xy)^{\land n} = \overbrace{xyxyxy\cdot\cdot\cdot xy}^{n} = \overbrace{xxx\cdot\cdot\cdot x}^{n}\ast \overbrace{yyy\cdot\cdot\cdot y}^n\\ = x^{\land n}y^{\land n}$$

Exercise: There's another law we can prove for exponentials, namely one for `(x^{\wedge y})^{\wedge z}`. What is the law and what is its proof?

Answer: The law is that this is equal to $$x^{\land yz}$$ The proof works similarly to how the previous one did.

$$(x^{\land y})^{\land z} = \overbrace{(x^{\land y})\cdot\cdot\cdot(x^{\land y})}^{z} = x^{\land \overbrace{y+y+...+y}^{z}}$$ where this last equality is true from what we proved in the previous exercise. From here we get $$= x^{\land yz}$$ from the definition of repeated addition as multiplication.

Question: What is the inverse of exponentiation?

Answer: There isn't one! Or rather: There isn't just one! There are two inverses of exponentiation, and this is due to the fact that exponentiation is not commutative.

To illustrate, take the relationship 3^2 = 9. The inverse should cancel something here, just like how in 5+1 the inverse -1 will cancel the +1 and leave just 5. If we choose to cancel the 2 and leave the 3, then we are using the root inverse, in particular the square-root (because it cancels the square). In Python you can enter

In[n]: sqrt(9)

Out[n]: 3

or you could also enter

In[n]: sqrt(3**2)

Out[n]: 3

Depending on whether you still have the SymPy session loaded by the command sp.init_session() you the output in the console may look slightly different from mine. If you have a session running, then if you enter sqrt(x) you'll get a pretty printing of the expression using the "surd" character, $$\sqrt{x}$$

SymPy also offers a cube-root function to cancel third powers.

In[n]: cbrt(1000)

Out[n]: 10

In Mathematical rather than CompSci contexts we write the cube-root as $$\sqrt[3]{x}$$

You can enter other roots with the command root(e, p) where e is the intended exponential value and p is the power. The standard mathematical notation is $$\sqrt[n]{x}$$

Finally there is the inverse which cancels the base, which we call the logarithm. Where sqrt(3**2) cancels the power and returns the base, 3; with the logarithm log(3, 3**2) cancels the base and returns 2, the power. I'll illustrate again on new numbers. If you haven't already, you'll likely need to initialize a SymPy session.

In[n]: log(5, 25)

Out[n]: 2

The general syntax in SymPy for logarithms is log(b,e) where b represents the base and e represents the exponential value.

Exercise: As we have done for subtraction and division, state the meaning of the root in terms of "The nth root of x is that number which, when ... results in ..." and do likewise for the logarithm base n.

Answer: The nth root of x is that number which, when raised to the nth power, results in x.

The logarithm base n is that number which, when put in the power of n results in x.

For examples, sqrt(16) = 4 and 4 is that number which when raised to the second power results in 16.

log(3,81) = 4 and 4 is that number which when put in the power of 3 results in 81.

Question: Are either the root or logarithm commutative, associative, distributive?

Answer: For commutative and associative, it's hard to pin down what these even mean for the square-root function. There is a sense we could give to the more general square function, but I fear I'll only confuse the reader at this stage, so for commutative and associative, let's leave it at "not really". But there is a sense in which distributive does make sense for both of them.

Starting with the root, and for specificity let's talk about the square-root. This does not distribute over addition: $$\sqrt{4+9}=\sqrt{13}$$ which is not even a well-defined number for us right now. On the other hand $$\sqrt{4}+\sqrt{9}=2+3$$ a totally different answer.

However, it seems to work with multiplication and it does. How do we prove it? That is to say, how do we show that, if x and y have square roots, then $$\sqrt{xy} = \sqrt{x}\sqrt{y}$$ One nice way to prove this is to assume it's true, see if we can move it to a form or place we like, and then try to work backwards! Assuming the equation is true we can square both sides and get

$$xy = (\sqrt{x}\sqrt{y})^{\land 2} = \sqrt{x}^{\land 2} \sqrt{y}^{\land 2} = xy$$

The end result, xy = xy, is clearly an equation we can believe. Now to back-track the process we just start here and work backwards.

Let's use the fact that $$xy = \sqrt{x}^{\land 2}\sqrt{y}^{\land 2} = (\sqrt{x}\sqrt{y})^{\land 2}$$

From here we can square-root both sides and get exactly what we want $$\sqrt{xy} = \sqrt{x}\sqrt{y}$$

A similar proof works for any root and power.

Now for the logarithm; what could we say about, for example, $$log(2, xy)$$ that is to say the logarithm-base-2 of xy? If we tinker and play with distribution over + and * it doesn't work. Maybe there is no relationship.

But here's another idea for a proof: Use something that seems relevant and make easy manipulations with logarithms to see if a law jumps out at you! What I have in mind is to take the law that we proved earlier (I re-label the numbers in order not to conflict with the numbers already used in this answer)

$$b^{\land p}b^{\land q} = b^{\land p+q}$$

If we just try to take the logarithm-base-b, what will we get? Because the logarithm cancels the base, then on the right-hand-side what we get is obviously just p+q.

$$\log(b, b^{\land p}b^{\land q}) = p+q$$

If you think hard enough about it, this really just is the law that we want! This tells us that

$$\log(b, b^{\land p}b^{\land q}) = p+q = \log(b, b^{\land p}) + \log(b, b^{\land q})$$

If we blot out b^p and just call this x, and likewise call b^q to be y then what this shows us is that the logarithm distributes over multiplication and turns it into addition!

$$\log(b, xy) = \log(b,x) + \log(b,y)$$

Exercise: Use the distributive law and the fact that 729 = 27*27 to effortlessly compute cbrt(729). Also use the derived rule for logarithms to easily compute log(2, 4^4).

Answer: cbrt(729) = cbrt(27)cbrt(27) = 3*3 = 9.

log(2, 4^4) = log(2, 4*4*4*4) = log(2, 4) + log(2, 4) + log(2, 4) + log(2, 4) = 2*2*2*2 = 16

Question: The previous solution for the logarithm problem indicates that there is another law we can derive, this time for log(b, x^y). What is it?

Answer:

$$\log(b, x^{\land y}) = \log(b, \overbrace{xxx\cdot\cdot\cdot x}^y) = \overbrace{\log(b,x) + ... + \log(b,x)}^y = y\log(b,x)$$

Hence, the law we can derive is $$\log(b,x^{\land y}) = y\log(b,x)$$
