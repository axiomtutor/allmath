Section 2: Adding and subtracting

Abstract: A base system makes adding and subtracting easy, especially in tabular form.

Question: How can we add numbers?

Answer: By organizing two numbers into groups of ones, tens, and so on, when we sum the digits we are so-to-speak summing similar terms. Summing groups of ones results in a count of simple units. Summing groups of tens results in a count of tens, etc. Thus to sum 987+65 we can sum just the units, 7+5, and get 12. We can sum the tens, 8+6 = 14. Since we can split off a group of 10 from the units, we can say that the sum has 9 groups of 100, 13 groups of 10, and 2 units. We can again split off a group of 10 tens, now call this 10 groups of 100, 3 tens, 2 units. Now split off again, and the result is 1 thousands, 0 hundreds, 3 tens, 2 units, or 1032. Organizing this process in a table makes it more readable and efficient.

Answer: When arguing that (1+1+1+1) + 9 + 99 + 999 + 9999 = (1+9) + (1+99) + (1+999) + (1+9999). If I did this step-by-step we'd have the intermediate use of commutativity (1+1+1+1) + 9 + 99 + 999 + 9999 = 1+1+1+9+1+99+999+9999 and then keep doing likewise until all the 1s are in place.

Question: The above argument also makes use of another property of addition, called associativity.

Definition: We say that addition is associative because x+(y+z) = (x+y)+z.

Where was associativity used and how could we justify its truth?

Answer: Of course the re-arranging of parentheses was also used, in addition to commutativity, in arguing that (1+1+1+1) + 9 + 99 + 999 + 9999 = (1+9) + (1+99) + (1+999) + (1+9999). To identify the exact point where it was used we would have to draw out all the implied intermediate steps, which is not terribly important right now.

Associativity is justified again by thinking of collections of dots. If we have three collections, it doesn't matter which two we combine first--the total is unaffected.

Note: Commutativity and associativity may seem so trivial as to be not worth discussing. I bring them up here, in a context where things are easy. We will eventually see examples were commutativity fails and so does associativity--in fact, both will fail very soon, in the context of subtraction. It will be more insightful to see them fail in more advanced contexts like matrices.

Question: What number, when added to 5, results in 12?

Answer: 7

This exercise can be seen in two useful ways. For one thing, it is the same as the solution of the equation x+5 = 12. We won't focus on this perspective much until discussing Algebra, but it's easy enough to understand now. From another perspective, it is the same as 12 minus 5, so that in a sense this defines the subtraction operation. Of course the interpretation of 12-5 is the amount that remains when 5 is taken away from 12, but it will be helpful to think of many inverse operations as the answer to a certain kind of question, like we have here. So in general we define

Definition: x-y is that number which, when added to y, results in x. x is called the minuend and y is called the subtrahend. x-y is called the difference.

Question: What is 123-45?

Answer: We can again make a table algorithm for computing the difference.

Definition: An algorithm is any fully specified sequence of steps that take some well-defined input and return a well-defined output.

But to come up with the algorithm, let's first think this out: We want to find the number, call it x, such that x+45=123. We could try to guess 100, but that's too big.

Note: These considerations illustrate that, what we want is the largest value of x that's not too large. Later on what we could call this is the infimum, or the "greatest lower-bound". For now I just want to drop in that perspective to warm you up to it--but we won't actually use it until much later. Why not the supremum, the smallest value not too small? In this case it amounts to the same thing, but the infimum perspective is more useful to "quickly" reach the answer. I won't explain why now, as this note is already long and I've introduced the concepts that I wanted to--we'll learn more about these ideas when discussing algorithms more specifically.

But clearly if we go down by an order, down to 10, then 10 is too small for x. 60 is an easy guess because 60+45 = 105. From there we can guess 20 extra, but of course this is too much: 20+60+45 = 125. So we ratchet down to 10+60+45 = 115, and of course give it another 5 and then another 3. In total

3+5+10+60+45 = 123

or more to the point

78+45 = 123

and therefore 123-45 = 78.

This algorithm is hard to formalize, but it's possible. However, we can tweak it to get a simpler table algorithm. The main problem is what to do when the digit below is larger than the digit above, like the 3 and the 9 in the difference 123-98.

Question: Why does this algorithm work? Can we give a proof of its correctness?

Answer: The proof is quite easy, which is to be expected given that the operation is quite basic. First, borrowing does not change the quantity being represented, it only changes the representation. Therefore nothing is lost in this step. Finally, since every digit-wise difference represents subtracting commensurable objects (e.g. 11 hundreds minus 9 hundreds makes sense and gives a count of how many hundreds remain after subtraction) this validly divides the problem into parts, performs the legitimate operation on the parts, and reassembles them at the end.

We could also justify the algorithm, once we understand multiplication and negatives adequately, by the principle that 123 - 98 = 110 + 13 - (90 + 8) = (110-90) + (13-8) = (11-9)*10 + 5 = 2*10 + 5 = 25.

Question: Why do we have no distinction between the right- and left-summand--both are simply called a "summand"--whereas for subtraction we distinguish the minuend and subtrahend?

Answer: The commutativity of addition implies that since x+y = y+x then the left-hand expression has no particular significance in addition. In subtraction, although 5-4 = 1, on the other hand 4-5 makes no sense with the interpretations we've been using so far. There is no natural number which, when added to 5, produces 4. This illustrates that subtraction is not commutative.

Question: Is subtraction associative?

Answer: A simple example like 3-2-1 will demonstrate that the answer is no. If we parenthesize it as 3-(2-1) = 3-1 = 2 we get a different answer from the other way of parenthesizing, (3-2)-1 = 1-1 = 0.

Question: Since subtraction expresses like 3-2-1 are ambiguous, how can we deal with the ambiguity?

Answer: One way is to simply parenthesize everything as above in order to explicitly rule out ambiguity.

Another way is establish a set of assumptions, like 3-2-1 is assumed to always mean 3-(2-1). This approach is common in Computer Science. To assume that 3-2-1 means 3-(2-1) is to say that we assume subtraction is right-associative. If we assume that 3-2-1 means (3-2)-1 then we are saying that we assume subtraction is left-associative. We will use these concepts later.

Another way that I personally like a whole lot more, which also makes an appearance in Computer Science, is to use Polish notation, also called prefix notation. We could write - 3 1 to mean three minus 1. Then we would say - 3 1 = 2. In that case - - 3 2 1 would have to compute to - 1 1. There's just no other meaningful interpretation of - - 3 2 1. To get the other version, we would write - 3 - 2 1 which would have to evaluate to - 3 1. This avoids the need for assumptions and the need for writing a whole lot of confusing parentheses when these expressions get long.

Polish notation also has the advantage that it gets you used to writing the function symbol at the front, which is exactly the same thing that you do for sin(x) or f(x). The function is written in the front and the inputs to the function are written afterwards. Most of common Math is written in prefix, function notation with the exceptions of the basic operations.

However, Polish notation comes with two significant disadvantages. For one thing, which is probably most readers' immediate response, it looks weird. You're not used to it. And it doesn't show up in most other Math contexts. Normally if there were enough advantage, I wouldn't heed that too much--you can learn to translate between the different notation systems--but Polish notation has another flaw. It's not graphical. In our usual notation system, we use graphical features like concatenation to represent multiplication: xy means x times y. We also use super-scripts for exponents and vertically stacked text separated by a bar for division. These really help readability for complex expressions and so I won't spend the energy trying to write these notes in Polish notation.

That said, I'll use left-associativity to solve the ambiguity problem. Thus 5 - 4 - 1 = 0. Still that won't stop me from giving at least one exercise in the use of Polish notation.
